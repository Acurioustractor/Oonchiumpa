# Video Editing Guide: Documentary Craft for Mission-Driven Storytelling

*A foundations document for editors of community, documentary, and Indigenous-led short-form work. Written for the Oonchiumpa project but generalisable.*

> **Provenance note.** This guide synthesises widely taught principles from Walter Murch's *In the Blink of an Eye* (2nd ed., Silman-James, 2001), Karen Pearlman's *Cutting Rhythms: Intuitive Film Editing* (2nd ed., Focal/Routledge, 2016), Michael Rabiger's *Directing the Documentary*, Sheila Curran Bernard's *Documentary Storytelling*, and published Indigenous screen protocols (AIATSIS GERAIS 2020; Screen Australia *Pathways & Protocols*, 2009, rev. 2023; First Nations Media Australia *Remote Media Toolkit*). Treat direct quotations as paraphrases unless verified against the cited editions; treat interpretive synthesis as inferred from those sources.

---

## TL;DR — The Five Principles to Edit By

1. **Cut on emotion first, story second, rhythm third** — and only then on continuity. This is Murch's "Rule of Six," and he weights it explicitly: **emotion is 51% of the decision; the three continuity rules combined are 7%.** If a cut honours what the audience should feel but breaks a continuity rule, keep the cut.

2. **Rhythm is the physical experience of the film, not a metronome.** Karen Pearlman frames editing as shaping three rhythms: **physical (bodies/movement), emotional (feeling-shape), event (story beats).** Before every cut, name which of the three you are serving.

3. **Hold shots longer than feels comfortable — especially on Country, and especially on Elders.** The viewer needs ~2–3s to recognise a new image, another 1–2s to read its emotional register, and more to form meaning. On-Country landscape held 6–12s with ambient sound is the shot doing the work the voiceover cannot.

4. **The J-cut is the documentary editor's primary tool for honouring voice.** Bring the next speaker's audio in *under* the current image (J-cut), or hold a speaker's audio *over* the next image (L-cut). In interview-and-B-roll docs, most cuts should be split-audio cuts, not hard cuts.

5. **Protocols are editing grammar, not post-production polish.** For Indigenous content, consent extends to the cut. Show the rough cut to community and Elders depicted **before locking picture**. (AIATSIS GERAIS Principle 2: *Respect*; Screen Australia *Pathways & Protocols* §4.)

## 1. Pacing and Cadence

### Core principle
Pearlman: *"Rhythm in film is created by the patterning of movement, energy, emotion and event in time."* Pacing is not "how fast cuts happen" — it is **the contour of the viewer's attention over time.**

### Rules you can apply on the timeline

- **The rule of three paces.** A documentary breathes when it operates at three speeds:
  - **Contemplative** — 6–15s per shot (landscape, arrival, grief, reflection)
  - **Narrative** — 3–6s per shot (interviews, exposition)
  - **Kinetic** — under 2s per shot (energy, dance, celebration, travel)

  A short doc that stays in one speed the whole time feels flat regardless of which speed.

- **Accelerate into meaning, decelerate out of it.** Cuts should *release* energy, not *generate* it. If the emotional payload of the next shot is large, slow the cuts *before* arriving; let the payload shot breathe *after* arriving.

- **Never cut on the downbeat of a sentence.** Cut on the *inhale* — the breath before a new thought. Scrub to the speaker's breath intake and cut 4–8 frames before it.

- **A scene should end one beat sooner than feels right.** Your instinct as the person who shot it will be to hold. Trust that the audience got it earlier than you did.

### For Oonchiumpa specifically
Open contemplative (Country, ambient, no voice). Move to narrative (Elder / young person speaking). Use kinetic sparingly — once, maybe twice, as a release. Return to contemplative to close. **Breath in → speak → breath out.**

## 2. Pauses and Silence

### Core principle
Murch: *"An edit is a blink. We blink to punctuate a thought."* Silence is the viewer's blink — the moment the film allows thought to form.

### Rules

- **The 3-second rule for silence after a significant line.** If an Elder says something that carries weight, sit with it for at least three seconds before moving on — either on their face, on a listener's face, or on the land. Cutting immediately signals that *you* didn't think it mattered.

- **Ambient sound is not silence.** True silence (no room tone) almost always reads as a technical error. "Silence" in documentary means *absence of voice and music while presence of place continues.* Always have a bed of location sound under every "silent" moment.

- **Silence loses the viewer at ~8–10 seconds without visual change.** A 12s landscape hold works if clouds are moving or light is shifting; the same hold on a static rock fails at 7.

- **Pair silence with a held face, not a held wide.** Emotional silence lives on a face. Environmental silence lives on a wide. Don't mix them.

### For interview + landscape B-roll
After a charged interview line, cut to a landscape with ambient sound for 4–8s *before* the next line. Single most effective trick in community documentary editing: *this was heard, and the land is holding it.*

## 3. Overlay Cadence (Text and Titles)

### Core principle
Every text overlay is a **second voice in the room.** Ask: is this voice necessary, or am I using it because I don't trust the footage?

### Rules

- **Three functions only.** Text should (1) **identify** (name, role, place, date), (2) **orient** (statistic, year, translation), or (3) **punctuate** (chapter break). Everything else, delete.

- **Lower-thirds: once per person, early, then never again.** Within 2–4s of first appearance. Don't reintroduce unless role has changed meaningfully.

- **Hold text for reading-time + 1 second.** ~3 words per second benchmark. A 9-word lower-third needs ~4s on screen. Shorter reads as anxious.

- **Never overlay text over a face delivering emotion.** The viewer cannot read and feel simultaneously.

- **Translations and language overlays for Indigenous-language content should be styled differently** — usually centred, slightly larger, held longer. Signals that translation is a form of respect.

- **Chapter titles work in fours, fives, or sevens — not threes.** Three in a short doc feels like a slide deck. Either none, or enough that structure becomes rhythm.

### Integration test
After a full pass, mute the audio and scrub. If the overlays by themselves tell a coherent "text story," you have too many.

## 4. B-Roll Rhythm and the Grammar of Cutaways

### Core principle
Murch: *"The key to good cutting is knowing what to leave out."* B-roll is not decoration over interview — it is the film's argument about what the words *mean*.

### Rules

- **J-cut into interviews, L-cut out of them.** Bring the speaker's voice in *under* a landscape or activity shot (J-cut), then reveal the speaker on-camera *after* we've already been listening for 2–3s. When exiting, let their voice continue over the next B-roll shot for 2–3s (L-cut). **Single most important mechanical habit in documentary editing.**

- **Cutaways have three purposes, not one.**
  1. **Compression** — cut "um", repetition, dead time
  2. **Illustration** — show what the speaker describes
  3. **Counterpoint** — show something that complicates, nuances, or resonates against what is said

  The third is the most powerful and least used. An Elder speaks of loss; we cut to children laughing. The juxtaposition carries what neither shot could alone.

- **Don't illustrate every noun.** If the Elder says "when I was a young girl on Country," you do *not* need to cut to a generic landscape. Imagination does that. Cut when the *thought* shifts, not when a concrete noun appears.

- **Match action, energy, or gaze.** When cutting between B-roll shots, honour one of: motion vector, energy level, or eye-line. Violating all three creates a jolt; violating one is invisible.

- **The 2-shot minimum.** Never use a single B-roll shot alone between two interview pieces — it reads as an interruption. Use at least two, or hold the one 6+ seconds.

- **Drone shots are punctuation, not paragraphs.** A drone reveal is a strong full-stop or chapter break. Using drone as general B-roll flattens its power. Budget: one or two drone moments in a short doc, at structural hinges.

## 5. Openings and Closings

### Core principle
The first 10 seconds make a promise. The last 10 seconds keep it or break it.

### Opening rules

- **Open on place, not person.** For community documentary, the first image should locate the viewer on Country. Craft (we need to know where the body is) and protocol (the land precedes the people).

- **Voice-over or voice-under should begin within the first 6–10 seconds.** Silent beautiful openings longer than 10s risk reading as a tourism reel. Let a voice enter early.

- **Do not put your logo first.** Opening branding pushes the film into ad-register. Hold logos for the end card or for a quiet moment 30–60s in.

- **State the film's question within 30 seconds.** Not its answer — its *question*. Every documentary is powered by a question the audience wants answered. If the first 30s don't plant a question, the film is a montage, not a story.

### Closing rules

- **Echo, don't summarise.** The strongest endings return to an image, phrase, or sound from the opening — but transformed by what the viewer now knows. A closing voiceover that summarises is almost always a cut you should make.

- **End on a held shot, not a cut.** Final image held 4–8s before fade or credits. Cutting directly from last line to black feels curt.

- **Leave one question open.** A short doc that resolves every question feels like a brochure. The best endings point outward.

- **Credits are part of the film.** 10–20s of sustained ambient under acknowledgements. Silence under credits is not the shape.

## 6. Cultural Sensitivity and Indigenous Media Protocols

> **Authoritative sources.** AIATSIS GERAIS (2020); Screen Australia *Pathways & Protocols* (Terri Janke); First Nations Media Australia *Remote Media Toolkit*; *Indigenous Cultural and Intellectual Property (ICIP) Protocol*.

### Principles that govern the edit

- **Consent is continuous, not one-off.** Consent to be filmed is not consent to be edited a particular way. Contributors — especially Elders — must see the rough cut and have meaningful input before picture lock. Build this review into your schedule.

- **Sorry Business.** If any person in the footage has passed away between shoot and edit, pause. Do not release without consultation with family and community about whether/how their image may be used, including warnings at the start of the film.

- **Country is not backdrop.** Do not use sacred or significant sites as generic B-roll. Drone footage especially — aerial views reveal sites that ground-level filming does not.

- **Do not score grief with music.** Music laid under an Elder speaking of loss, stolen generations, or dispossession frames their testimony as a feeling to be managed rather than a truth to be heard. Voice + ambience alone.

- **Language matters in order.** Whose voice opens, whose image is largest, whose name appears first in credits — these are read as statements of authority. Community-controlled organisations typically want Elders' voices early, youth voices central, organisational voices later or not at all.

- **Avoid the "deficit frame" cut.** Cutting from an Elder speaking of strength to footage of disadvantage (empty houses, struggling landscapes) undercuts the Elder. Cut instead to footage of the strength they are describing.

- **Holding space for Country without ornamenting it.** Landscape shots graded minimally (no heavy teal-orange, no vignettes, no speed ramps), held 6–12s, with *that place's* ambient sound — not stock wind, not library birds. Land is a character.

- **Warnings and acknowledgements are part of the edit, not metadata.** The opening "may contain images and voices of people who have passed away" warning and acknowledgement of Country are **edit decisions**. Give each its own beat.

## 7. Music and Voice

### Core principle
Ambient sound creates **presence** (we are here). Music creates **interpretation** (feel this about being here). The editor decides when the film is observing and when it is interpreting.

### Rules

- **Voice first, then decide.** Edit the interview spine with no music. Only after the story works on voice alone, experiment. Music laid early masks editing problems; problems return when music is pulled.

- **Ambient carries voice better than music does.** Well-recorded location ambience under the speaker — wind, distant voices, fire crackle — adds more presence and credibility than any score.

- **The rule of entrance and exit.** Music should enter on a visual or structural event (a cut, location shift, new chapter) and exit the same way. Music fading up mid-sentence calls attention to itself.

- **Duck music under voice by at least 9–12 dB.** If you can hum along while someone is speaking, it is too loud. Music under voice is *felt*, not *heard*.

- **Silence between music cues is as important as the cues themselves.** Continuous music has no dynamic range. Let music end, let ambient take over, let voice carry alone — then re-enter for the next movement.

- **Choose music that does not impose a culture onto the film.** Generic "ethnic" or "cinematic-emotional" library tracks impose a frame. Prefer music made by Indigenous artists, ideally from the community depicted, with permission. Otherwise: ambient/textural beds over melodic tracks with cultural signalling.

- **Voice stands alone in three moments.**
  1. Opening line, if it's a strong statement from an Elder or central voice
  2. Any line about grief, loss, or truth-telling
  3. Final line before end-credits

## Appendix A — Editing Order of Operations

For a builder editing their own footage for the first time, order matters more than any single technique.

1. **Watch all footage once without cutting.** Make an inventory of moments (timecode + description). No timeline yet.
2. **Transcribe interviews.** Edit the *transcript* first — on paper or in a doc — into a spine of 8–15 lines. Rabiger's paper editing.
3. **Build the audio spine.** In the NLE, assemble interview-only cut. No B-roll, no music. If the story works on voice alone, proceed.
4. **Layer B-roll with J/L cuts.** Cover ugly cuts; add counterpoint; let voice lead image.
5. **Add ambient beds under everything.** Every frame should have location sound.
6. **Test with music sparingly.** Try without music first. Add only where absence is weaker than presence.
7. **Add overlays last.** Resist until picture is locked.
8. **Show to community / contributors before export.** Build time for meaningful revision.
9. **Sleep on it.** The film you see tired at night and the film you see rested in the morning are different films. The gap is where the final notes live.
